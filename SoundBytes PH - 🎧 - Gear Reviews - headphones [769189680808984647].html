DROP HIFIMAN HE5XX REVIEW Build: I was expecting a lot more from the build for $220. Judging from pictures prior to getting it in-hand, it is drastically lighter than it looks and it also feels LESS premium to the touch than it looks. However, after putting them on your head and long listening sessions, you will then appreciate the weight or lack thereof. Comfort: The 5XX has barely to no clamp force at all straight out of the box. The lack of clamp force and the lightweight construction makes these cans a longevity warrior during long listening sessions. I could go hours on end behind my desk without having to air my ears out and barely experience any neck fatigue like you do with a traditional heavy planar. The very GENEROUS diameter of the tear-drop shaped ear pad surrounds my ears perfectly and there is no contact with the inner walls of the ear pads. The earpad is constructed as a hybrid pleather and cloth that comes in contact with your temple. (edited)
07:55
Sound: The airyness and soundstage of these cans are the star of the show here. The very polar opposite of intimacy. In terms of sound signature, these cans ride the line between neutral and analytical sounding to my ears, but leans more to a neutral and flat tonality. Crispy on the highs, very forward and cold. Very emphasized and fast on the top end and still manages to stage well on the bass. Despite that, if you’re a bass head, stay away from the 5XX, although you may appreciate its very lean low-end delivery. But really the sound signature is not what I want to focus here because the airy characteristics of its soundstaging is what is in the spotlight. Listening to “Sisters of Arequipa” by Nils Landgren on the 5XX, genuinely made me feel like I was part of the funk unit playing amongst them. Competition and Conclusion: At the same price point as the 6XX at $220 USD, the only thing the 5XX does better is soundstage and detail, whereas better timbre and tonality can be found in the 6xx. If you are curious to hear what good soundstage is then these might be a good candidate to get a taste for that in the 200 dollar price range. But overall as a headphone, it's two only redeeming qualities might not be able to put it on top of it's competition. (edited)

SENNHEISER HD58X JUBILEE REVIEW TLDR: Get a 6xx instead lol Build: The build is not anything new, classic HD600 series build. The plastic feels cheap but very robust, I can comfortably throw this around without the slightest bit of care if I want to, but I take care of my stuff. The headband is finished with a glossy coating and the driver chassis with matte uncoated plastic. Very modular design, as the pads and driver grilles are easily popped on and off if you choose to replace pads or remove the foam behind the drivers. No quality control issues, no complaints. Overall the build is solid but not anything premium feeling to the touch. Comfort: The clamp force straight out of the box is unbearable. 1.4 lbs of force crushing your head feels like a vise in between your skull. They are the polar opposite of what SHP9500 or 5XX feels like, where those cans float over your ears and head, with barely any clamp at all. I recommend putting them over a block overnight to accelerate the break in process. The spring steel headband is very stubborn so the break in period will take some time. Other than that, the velour pads are very comfortable but the foam rigidity is very subpar, they have collapsed within a few months of use and have become lopsided. Replacement Drop pads are more comfortable but changed sound signature (more on that later). As with any stock HD600 pads, you might have to replace them on the regular, but the velour itself is very comfortable against the skin but it will pick up every dust or debri around your workstation, so keep your desk clean! Sound: The sound focuses on the mid-range and centralized vocals with a warm sound signature but sounds very lush. Warm cans, not too offensive on the low-end while still delivering ok detail on the sub-bass, but when it comes to the midrange and further up the FR thats where things become a little bit grainy. Sorta reminds me of 5x0 series Sennheiser detail which is not that good. The HD58x is a very intimate headphone, very narrow and centralized which helps with the vocals but lacking in soundstage. If you’re looking for soundstage, look elsewhere. Despite that, it isn't fatiguing to the ears after long sessions, it is not wide but it's not claustrophobic either. A modification I did that heavily altered the sound, is replacing the stock pads with Drop’s replacement pads. The reason for the replacement is that the foam integrity of the stock OEM pads are sub-par, and have become lopsided within a few months that will affect the seal of the headphones. The new Drop pads have turned the HD58x from a warm to a dark sound signature, it softens up the mids and highs to the point that I am looking for the detail that was there, but with these pads on, it is GONE. Upwards of 300hz everything sounds like its buried in sand essentially. I do not recommend getting third-party pads for any HD600 cans at all, stick with stock Sennheiser pads. The sound I can best describe as a warmer 650 with worse mids and subpar detail of 5 series Sennheisers. Conclusion: The HD58x is not my preference for how it sounds sonically and also I do believe that it is not worth the asking price of $170 USD. Other options down the Sennheiser lineup such as the 6XX is alot more headphone for a little bit more, and overall a better investment (review soon). Given that the 6XX exists, makes the 58x a hard recommend despite the 50 dollar price difference. (edited)

Mid-2000s Sparkle: AUDIO-TECHNICA ATH-ES7 (RIDICULOUSLY LONG) REVIEW ️DISCLAIMER:️ -This is my first ever written review of an audio gear. Although I've never had any other headphones to compare this against, I'll try my best to give a comprehensive review for these. INTRODUCTION -I've been on the hunt for a headphone that sounds great for only below $30 USD. Why only $30 you ask? Because that's all that I have on my wallet... I don't have a job in order to support my hobbies atm. Boohoo. I've heard good things about the Samson SR850s and the Koss Porta-Pros which have been regarded to be budget wonders. However, there two problems which made me don't wanna buy them: Based on the pictures, I find the SR850s to be too big and bulky, and I'm quite paranoid about people listening to me jamming to sh*t I listen to, so open-backs are a big no-no. And given my abusive nature of handling my own stuffs, I don't think the shoddy build quality of the Koss PeePees are gonna last for an entire week of my abuse either. So, is there anything that I can buy that sounds equally as great, is equally durable but doesn't have to force me to get a loan? (I hate loaning btw) So I looked up on Paphi- Marketplace for any offerings and I found this guy selling a bunch of old stock on a Pull-Out Sale, and there I saw it: A pair of white Audio-Technica ATH-ES7s displayed in the middle, with its white plastic all yellowed with age from sitting in the display shelves for too long. Then, I figured that I must buy them, and buy them I did..... At a price of only Php1,399 or 27 USD. And I'm a bit glad that I did. (edited)

A week later, these finally landed on my doorstep, encased in their original dark grey Audio-Technica box that looked like they were dragged along the rubble of an urban warzone somewhere in Iraq. I checked the manufacturing date of these labelled on the underside of the box- it was manufactured in late of May, 2009. After inspecting the box I finally unleashed the headphones from their carton coffin. Despite the battered condition of the box, the headphones are pretty much in okay condition, with no broken or loose parts, albeit some minor scratches on the outside face of its earcups which were polished to a mirror finish. BUILD QUALITY: Talking about build quality, these headphones are surprisingly built well: the shiny mirror earcups are actually made of stainless steel, and they swivel around in a 120-degree arc. Imo these look gorgeous and will definitely turn people's heads around but the thought of these getting deep scratches and fingerprints overtime is just too much for me to take. The white plastic is made out of solid ABS. Although they had a bit of yellowing, this is just an aesthetic wear, and to me personally, I think it adds a bit of character to the headphone. The cable is a bit on the thin, weak side, with the same rubbery feel as the cables on Apple dirtybuds, although it does have a nice strain relief on the plug. The earpads are 70mm in diameter and made out of cloth. I assumed that these actually used to have a pleather layer on top but has long since flaked away through the years. Nevertheless they provide decent cushioning, I can wear them for about 2 hours before my ears started aching. The headband is made out of thick silicone rubber, and it has stainless steel wires on either side that go through the entire rubber headband. They also give an audible click per increment of adjustment. The first thing I did with these is to cover the metal earcups with vinyl wallpaper sticker wrap. Then I bought newer and better pads. I just bought the thick pleather pads that had a faux fur on the inside of the cups. These have a drastic improvement over the stock ones in terms of sound isolation and comfort. (edited)
A Shiny Sucrose 23-Aug-21 01:27 AM
SOUND QUALITY: Long story short, these sound balanced-ish with a little touch of brightness for some sparkle. A. BASS -Right off the bat, I'll say this: THIS IS NOT A BASSY HEADPHONE. If you're someone who likes heaps of bass on your music, stop reading this and look elsewhere. But otherwise if you're looking fo something that's not really bassy, then keep reading on. Bass on the Audio-Technica ATH-ES7 is fast, tight and light. As I was listening 50 Cent- Candy Shop , Ruth B- Dandelions or Earth, Wind & Fire- Boogie Wonderland, I find that the bass does has some prescence to the music and it punches pretty quickly. Mid-bass never bleeds out into the lower mids as well which can make the overall sound signature a bit on the lean and boring side. Moreover, note weight is pretty much quite light. There is a bit of sub-bass, but you would really have to listen attentively to really pick it out. Overall, bass is present, but it is pushed far back into the music and doesn't take your attention. Definitely not for bassheads or for people looking for a warm sound signature, although personally I actually welcome this sort of bass, as I've already had enough IEMs and earbuds that can cater to my bassy needs anyway. B. MIDS -This, in my opinion, takes the spotlight in the Audio-Technica's tuning. Mids just sound forward and very sweet out of these! Listening to Adele - All I Ask is just enough to make me wanna replay it on loop. Her voice is just so crystal-clear and articulate with lots of texture and detail. Same can be said as I was listening to something like Westlife- Flying Without Wings and Kenny Rogers- Lady. Everything does have a bit of a lean timbre to it but still sounds natural nonetheless, and it doesn't really come to being shouty or fatiguing whatsoever. (edited)
01:28
The lack of mid-bass bleed does give the ATH-ES7s lack of warmth, although they did sound more warm and balanced when I plugged them into my laptop with a Jcally JA04 DAC dongle. C. TREBLE -Treble is the next best thing about the ATH-ES7s. They are ever so slightly elevated than the rest of the frequencies to add that touch of sparkle to the music, but they nailed it in terms of avoiding it sounding harsh or sibilant. I tried listening to Avril Lavigne- Happy Ending, as she does have sharp "S"es that can pierce my ears when I listen to it using earphones with aggressive treble (ie. my trashy i12 TWS) and so far I've been able to pull through without coming close to a smidge of treble earrape. Other than that, snares sound like they do in real life, cymbal crashes sounded lifelike with an almost spot-on sizzle. The sounds of the shaker from Michael Jackson- Billie Jean has perfect decay too, I have zero complaints with the ATH-ES7's treble. AUDIO-TECHNICA ATH-ES7 FR GRAPH: (edited)

SOUNDSTAGING, IMAGING AND SEPARATION -FYI, this is a closed-back headphone. You're not gonna get a wide soundstage out of these. However, none of the music tracks sound quite congested at any point in the track, I can listen to the likes of Dragonforce- Through The Fire And Flames or Reol- Q? with these, no problem. Closing my eyes does reveal the ATH-ES7 to have a soundstage the size of a small music studio, with equal width and depth. Layering and instrument separation is also above average for a closed-back, and I'm impressed how these can hold up to busy tracks with a small congestion to the busiest ones in my library. Imaging is somewhat okay. With a few listening sessions of Virtual Barber Shop Haircut, I can pick out stuff going from both sides, as well as front and back. Paired with the ability of these to dish out quite a bit of detail, the crinkling sound of the plastic bag draped over my head is enough to make my spine tingle in a pleasant way. COMFORT AND ISOLATION -With the stock pads on, the comfort that these headphones provide isn't great. Clamping force out of the box is a little tight as well. Swapping the earpads for something a bit plushier. From what I've heard, these can take earpads up to 90mm in diameter. Also, bending the headband outwards at the "shoulders" should ease up on the clamping force too. The headband has no padding on it and is a bit on the uncomfortable side. I got around this issue by grabbing a piece of neoprene that is used to protect the chainstays of mountain bikes. These along with the thicker earpads made these super comfortable to wear. These have decent noise isolation, although I can still hear loud noises and these leak a bit of sound. If you're a stranger standing 4 feet away from me while using these in a quiet room you're gonna hear everything, although you won't be able to in a more public setting with lots of background noise. OVERALL VERDICT If you're looking for a not-too-bassy headphone for cheap, get this! This used to retail at around 170USD in its heyday, so I highly advise against buying it at its original buying price as you can find heaps of better offerings at that price point nowadays. It's got the bling, it sounds as good as it looks, it's compact enough to be taken outdoors where you can't do the same with your studio monitor cans and it's easy to drive as well with its 32-ohm impedance. Plus, the build quality is good enough to stand up to everyday abuse. So far, I've had a blast with these headphones. These will be a permanent member to my collection and I will never ever sell these. I'd definitely do a cable mod on these when the cable fails on me later in the future. Thank you for reading! Audio Technica ESW9LTD REVIEW: TLDR: Get these if you don’t like the feel of IEMs or just want the AT house sound at its finest, build quality and comfort isn’t that good but the sound is unique and overall just amazing, and definitely the main selling point of these. Build: These headphones are well-built enough, with a carbon plastic chassis comprising its structure aside from the teak wood cups. The plastic itself feels great, light and textured. The cups themselves are glossy and beautiful, but don’t exude quality to the touch. Compared to the ATH-W1000, the wood feels thinner and less bulky. Its cable is thin and overall not so nice, to the extent that if this wasn't a limited edition, they would be MMCX modded by now. Overall, they are great for the price I got them at, but probably not worth the MSRP when it comes to build quality. Comfort: To be frank, the comfort of these headphones is subpar, unlike their over-ear counterparts. The oddly shaped pads means that they don't rest flat on your head and create some hot zones on your head. However, the pads do remain cool and don't cause moisture. Thin padding on the headband means that the metal band supporting it can be felt after around an hour of usage, though it's not to the point where it bothers me. https://cdn.discordapp.com/attachments/755233688425594901/884959461452689438/image1.jpg

Cont... Sound: Before I go into how these headphones sound, two different sources were used: Ipad Air 2 with 16/44 lossless Apple Music > Apple Dongle, and Bluesound Node 2 > Pioneer SA-7800. The soundstage is overall very spacious, with an uncommonly light and airy property to it, something that is possibly fueled by the excellent upper mids. The upper mids stand out the most in a good way, with a forward, clear, and certainly prominent presentation that I haven't heard in a headphone of this caliber and price range. Although the bass that this headphone produces isn't the deepest, but it is well-controlled and clear throughout the range. Something that stands out with it would be its tactility, which I find to be overall very pleasing. It's not the main selling point of this headphone but certainly good regardless. The treble feels overly bright and artificial in comparison to my ATH-A900, and have a small peak somewhere in the middle. It's not grainy however, and is not overly fatiguing for me. Something to note is that the driver does not feel fast, and as a result fast-paced music tends to blur together, but it's still manageable. Something that certainly shines here are string instruments, which feel like they carry weight and clarity. Driven off an Apple dongle, the sound retains all of the qualities described here, while through my amp, the sound becomes warmer and more engaging, quelling the artificial treble. Overall, the upper mids are really the main appeal for this headphone, and make me love it more than my IEMs. https://cdn.discordapp.com/attachments/755094291855966341/884962585907200080/image0.jpg

It'll be replacing my IEMs for sure, and I hope this review helps.
